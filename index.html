Main Dishes

Borscht (sometimes written as borsch, borsht, bortsch, or borshch)

A beetroot soup with vegetables often served with sour cream and fresh dill. A symbol of Ukrainian cuisine.

In 2022, UNESCO listed borscht as an item of Intangible Cultural Heritage in need of urgent safeguarding, recognizing its cultural importance.

Kapusniak

soup made with cabbage, pork, salo, beans, and served with sour cream.

Rosolnyk

soup with pickled cucumbers.

Yushka

clear soup; the most common variety — fish yushka is made from various types of fish such as carp, bream, wels catfish, or even ruffe.

Varenyky

Dumplings filled with meat, potatoes, mushrooms, vegetables, fruits, cheese, or berries. Their fillings can be salty or sweet, lean or meaty.

Holubtsi

Cabbage rolls stuffed with ground meat, rice, and onions, stewed in a tomato sauce, and served with sour cream.

Porridge

made out of buckwheat, wheat, barley, rye, millet, rice, oat, or corn.

Sides & Salads

Sauerkraut

fermented cabbage and carrots with a hefty sprinkle of salt.

Vinigret

Cold salad of beetroot, potatoes, pickles, carrots, sauerkraut and peas.

Salo

Cured pork fat served with garlic and rye bread. A classic Ukrainian snack.

Desserts

Syrnyky

Soft pancakes made with fresh cheese (*tvorog*), served with jam or sour cream.

Cherry Varenyky

Filled with sour cherries for a sweet and tart taste — not the sweet cherries you're used to!

About Ukrainian Food Culture

Ukrainian cuisine reflects a rural, family-based culture. Unlike in Spain, restaurants and bars are not part of daily life — most meals are cooked and eaten at home.

The harsh winters and fertile land have shaped a menu based on seasonal, preserved, and hearty ingredients like beets, potatoes, grains, and sour dairy products.
